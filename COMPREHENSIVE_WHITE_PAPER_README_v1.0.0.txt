PROJECT OVERVIEW
================================================================================

This comprehensive white paper analyzes whether formal survey instruments are
necessary when extensive qualitative interviews have already been conducted
with Indigenous cooperatives. The analysis spans two rounds of interviews with
seven total cooperatives and provides definitive evidence that surveys are
unnecessary for cooperatives that have completed comprehensive interviews.

DATA QUALITY:

Beyond coverage percentages, interviews provide:
‚Ä¢ Contextual richness (WHY and HOW, not just WHAT)
‚Ä¢ Cultural nuance (Indigenous values, languages, protocols)
‚Ä¢ Unexpected insights (issues not anticipated in survey design)
‚Ä¢ Honest assessment (candid discussion of challenges)
‚Ä¢ Voice and agency (Indigenous leaders' own narratives)

METHODOLOGICAL INSIGHTS:

Interview methodology demonstrates:
‚Ä¢ Robustness across cooperative types
‚Ä¢ Flexibility as strength (not limitation)
‚Ä¢ Efficiency paradox (richer data, less burden overall)
‚Ä¢ Alignment with Indigenous research methodologies

ETHICAL CONSIDERATIONS:

Strong case against redundant surveys:
‚Ä¢ Minimizes research burden on over-researched communities
‚Ä¢ Values knowledge already shared
‚Ä¢ Respects Indigenous ways of knowing
‚Ä¢ Builds rather than damages relationships
‚Ä¢ Honors principle of proportionality

================================================================================
PRIMARY RECOMMENDATION
================================================================================

DO NOT ADMINISTER FORMAL SURVEYS TO INDIGENOUS COOPERATIVES THAT HAVE
COMPLETED COMPREHENSIVE QUALITATIVE INTERVIEWS.

Rationale:
1. Interview data provides 97% HIGH/MEDIUM coverage of survey questions
2. Interview quality exceeds what surveys would provide
3. Surveys would create burden without commensurate benefit
4. Ethical principle of minimizing extraction from Indigenous communities
5. Interviews better align with Indigenous knowledge sharing practices

This recommendation is supported by:
‚Ä¢ Systematic analysis of 7 cooperatives
‚Ä¢ 63 question-response combinations
‚Ä¢ Evidence from diverse cooperative types and contexts
‚Ä¢ Consistent patterns across two rounds of analysis
‚Ä¢ Transparent, replicable methodology
